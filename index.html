Part A

Critical Evaluation of 10 Technology Tools

Each tool is evaluated for pedagogical value, NESA alignment, ethical considerations, and APST Standards — grounded in Stage 5 Mathematics (Years 9–10) and verified peer-reviewed research (2018–2026).

The ten tools evaluated in this portfolio represent a deliberate selection grounded in pedagogical evidence rather than novelty. Each tool was chosen because it serves a specific pedagogical purpose in the Stage 5 Mathematics classroom — enabling me as the teacher to demonstrate a specific APST Standard through the way I use it. All research cited is peer-reviewed and published between 2018 and 2026.

Tool 01 · APST 2.1 & 2.6
GeoGebra
🔗 geogebra.org
Visit GeoGebra ↗

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10

MA5.2-9NA (Non-Linear Relationships)

Free dynamic mathematics platform. Students can play with live graphs and geometric objects — converting abstract Stage 5 material into a mathematical space to investigate. Zulnaidi et al. (2020) confirmed significant improvement in functions when GeoGebra was used with 80 high school students.

Strengths

  • Conceptual depth and differentiation across the full ability range
  • Strong numeracy integration working with coordinates, functions and gradients
  • Free, cross-platform, and works offline — equity-positive for all NSW school contexts

Limitations & Risks

  • Cognitive overload if applets expose too many parameters simultaneously
  • High teacher preparation time to design quality applets
  • Privacy: student accounts require GDPR school approval

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

No advertising; no accounts required for basic use. Teacher must verify mathematical accuracy of all applets against NESA outcomes before use. School privacy policy approval required before creating student accounts.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standards 2.1 & 2.6

APST 2.1 (content knowledge) and 2.6 (ICT to expand learning). Designing a dynamic exploration demonstrates higher-order pedagogical content knowledge than static instruction (Ziatdinov & Valles, 2022).

📚 Research Support

Zulnaidi, H., Oktavika, E., & Hidayat, R. (2020). Education and Information Technologies, 25(1), 51–72. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10639-019-09899-y

Ziatdinov, R., & Valles, J. R. (2022). Mathematics, 10(3), 398. https://doi.org/10.3390/math10030398

Tool 02 · APST 1.2 & 1.5
Desmos Activity Builder
🔗 teacher.desmos.com
Visit Desmos ↗

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10

MA5.1-6NA (Linear Relationships); MA5.2-9NA (Non-Linear Relationships)

Multi-screen interactive lesson platform with real-time teacher dashboard showing every student's graph and response. Chechan et al. (2023) found statistically significant improvement in function understanding (n=98 secondary students) when Desmos was used.

Strengths

  • Real-time individual formative data — reveals misconceptions as they form, not after a test
  • Self-paced differentiation; no student accounts required; students join via class code
  • Live graphing environment with strong numeracy integration

Limitations & Risks

  • Requires reliable internet per student — equity barrier in lower-SES settings
  • Significant teacher design investment required for quality activities
  • Distraction risk without structured screen parameters

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

Data stored on US servers but minimal PII collected as students join without accounts. Anonymised sharing protects students during class discussion. No advertising served to students in classroom sessions.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standards 1.2 & 1.5

APST 1.2 (understand how students learn) and 1.5 (differentiate for diverse learners). Real-time dashboard enables within-lesson evidence-based instructional response (Chechan et al., 2023).

📚 Research Support

Chechan, B., Ampadu, E., & Pears, A. (2023). Eurasia Journal of Mathematics, Science and Technology Education, 19(10), em2331. https://doi.org/10.29333/ejmste/13540

Tool 03 · APST 5.1 & 5.4
Kahoot!
🔗 kahoot.com
Visit Kahoot! ↗

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10

All Stage 5 content strands — diagnostic and revision tool

Game-based formative assessment platform. Post-game item-level analytics identify specific concepts requiring reteaching. Wang and Tahir (2020) reviewed 93 studies and confirmed positive effects on learning performance when post-game data is actively used by teachers.

Strengths

  • High engagement; post-game item analytics drive evidence-based instructional decisions
  • Game-PIN join requires no student accounts — minimal data collection
  • Brief 10–15 minute format fits any lesson structure

Limitations & Risks

  • Time pressure disadvantages EAL/D and anxious learners — inaccurate assessment data
  • Multiple-choice cannot assess reasoning or proof
  • Gamification risks shifting motivation from learning to winning

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

Game-PIN minimises data collection; GDPR-compliant servers (Norway). Team mode must be used to prevent public humiliation of low performers. AI question builder requires teacher review for mathematical accuracy before classroom use.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standards 5.1 & 5.4

APST 5.1 (assessment strategies) and 5.4 (interpret data to modify teaching). Post-game analytics enable the evidence-based instructional decisions Standard 5.4 requires (Wang & Tahir, 2020).

Tool 04 · APST 4.1 & 4.4
Padlet
🔗 padlet.com
Visit Padlet ↗

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10

MA5.2-2WM (Select strategies); MA5.2-3WM (Reasoning and justification)

Moderated collaborative digital board. Students share problem-solving approaches without public vulnerability. Naamati-Schneider and Alt (2023) found that Padlet-mediated collaboration significantly increased perceived participation safety compared to face-to-face contribution.

Strengths

  • Teacher-moderated posting prevents harmful peer responses
  • Anonymous option reduces mathematics anxiety; increases inclusive participation
  • Literacy integration via written justifications aligned to MA5.2-3WM
  • Formative data on all students including the quietest

Limitations & Risks

  • Free tier limits to 3 boards — constraint for multi-unit sustained use
  • Delayed moderation disrupts lesson flow
  • Open format risks off-task behaviour without clear task parameters

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

Guest posting requires no accounts (COPPA-compliant for 13+). Teacher must explicitly disclose to students that all posts, including anonymous ones, are visible through the moderation dashboard. Students must not post personally identifiable information.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standards 4.1 & 4.4

APST 4.1 (inclusive participation) and 4.4 (safe interactions). Moderated anonymous board creates the psychological safety that Standard 4.1 requires (Naamati-Schneider & Alt, 2023).

Tool 05 · APST 3.3, 3.4 & 3.6
Nearpod
🔗 nearpod.com
Visit Nearpod ↗

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10

MA5.2-12MG (Surface Area and Volume) — applicable across all Stage 5 strands

Complete lesson delivery platform embedding tasks, polls, and formative checkpoints within a structured sequence. Hakami (2020) found Nearpod-integrated lessons produced significantly higher active learning outcomes than traditional delivery (n=74 students).

Strengths

  • Multi-strategy lesson delivery in one platform — planning, delivery, and evaluation combined
  • Self-paced differentiation; real-time individual dashboard data
  • Lesson data supports post-lesson evaluation and program improvement

Limitations & Risks

  • Free tier restricts lesson size — school subscription required for sustained use
  • Home internet required for self-paced mode — equity concern
  • AI content builder requires teacher verification for mathematical accuracy

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

Data stored on US servers — school data governance must be confirmed before use. Student accounts managed through teacher-controlled class codes. AI-generated lesson content must always be teacher-verified before student use — mathematically inaccurate AI content in class is an ethical failure.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standards 3.3, 3.4 & 3.6

APST 3.3 (teaching strategies), 3.4 (select resources), and 3.6 (evaluate programs). Nearpod is simultaneously a planning, delivery, and evaluation tool — addressing all three sub-standards (Hakami, 2020).

Tool 06 · APST 2.6
Wolfram Alpha
🔗 wolframalpha.com
Visit Wolfram Alpha ↗

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10 (extension and verification)

MA5.3-7NA (Real Numbers); MA5.3-8NA (Polynomials); MA5.3-14MG (Trigonometry)

Computational knowledge engine used as a post-solution verification tool. Students complete problems by hand, then check their working against Wolfram's step-by-step output. Ziatdinov and Valles (2022) confirm computational tools expand mathematical inquiry beyond what is accessible by hand.

Strengths

  • Verifies multi-step calculations and shows step-by-step working
  • Supports extension inquiry beyond the core Stage 5 syllabus
  • Rewards reasoning when used as checker; free with no account required

Limitations & Risks

  • High misuse risk — students may bypass mathematical thinking entirely
  • Overuse erodes manual computation skills required in NESA assessments
  • Query data retained by Wolfram Research

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

Academic integrity is the primary ethical concern. Explicit classroom protocols and a dedicated digital citizenship lesson are required before any student use. The tool must be framed as a post-solution checker, never an answer generator. Students should not include personal information in queries.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standard 2.6

APST 2.6 (ICT to expand learning). Expands Stage 5 mathematical inquiry range under explicit teacher-guided academic integrity protocols (Ziatdinov & Valles, 2022).

Mathematics — Stage 5, cross-curricular

MA5.2-3WM (Reasoning and justification); Literacy General Capability

Free graphic design platform used for authentic multimodal production tasks. Students design mathematical infographics, developing written and visual communication alongside content knowledge. Naamati-Schneider and Alt (2023) confirm digital production tasks increase student engagement and learning ownership.

COPPA/FERPA-compliant; no advertising on school accounts. Students must not upload personal photographs. AI authorship must be explicitly addressed as an academic integrity lesson before any assessed task — the distinction between AI-assisted design and original student work must be clear.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standards 3.3 & 2.5

APST 3.3 (repertoire of strategies) and 2.5 (literacy and numeracy strategies). Multimodal production integrates mathematical and literacy outcomes, supported by evidence of increased learning ownership (Naamati-Schneider & Alt, 2023).

Khan Academy
🔗 khanacademy.org

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10 (consolidation and revision)

All Stage 5 content strands — supplementary self-paced practice

Free non-profit mastery-based practice platform with adaptive exercises and a teacher dashboard. Used for consolidation and homework. Vidergor and Ben-Amram (2020) found secondary mathematics students perceived themselves as more independent learners and found the platform more accessible than textbooks.

Strengths

  • Free, no advertising, accessible on low-spec devices — most equitable tool in this portfolio
  • Adaptive practice engine personalises exercises; comprehensive teacher dashboard
  • Embedded video hints develop mathematical vocabulary alongside procedural skills

Limitations & Risks

  • Plain interface risks disengagement in extended practice sessions
  • Requires continuous internet — barrier for students without home Wi-Fi

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

Non-profit privacy stance — data not sold or used for advertising; COPPA-compliant. Khanmigo AI tutor requires explicit school opt-in and teacher verification before any student use. Teacher presence must remain central — the platform supplements but must not replace the teacher-student relationship.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standards 1.5 & 5.1

APST 1.5 (differentiate teaching) and 5.1 (assessment strategies). Adaptive engine provides personalised practice at scale; teacher dashboard supports formative planning (Vidergor & Ben-Amram, 2020).

Tool 09 · APST 5.2
Quizizz
🔗 quizizz.com
Visit Quizizz ↗

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10

All Stage 5 content strands — revision and formative practice

Asynchronous gamified quiz platform with explanatory post-question feedback. Unlike Kahoot, Quizizz removes time pressure and explains why each answer is correct. Wang and Tahir (2020) found feedback quality is the key variable determining whether game-based tools produce genuine learning gains.

Strengths

  • Explanatory feedback tells students why an answer is correct — not just right/wrong
  • Self-paced removes time pressure — more equitable for EAL/D and anxious learners
  • Game elements maintain engagement; no accounts required for guest play

Limitations & Risks

  • Multiple-choice cannot assess reasoning, proof, or multi-step problem-solving
  • Default meme insertion can be culturally inappropriate — must always be disabled
  • Student responses stored on US servers — school data governance must be verified

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

Minimal data for guest play. Meme feature must be disabled — algorithmically selected memes can be culturally insensitive. Student performance data must be used formatively and never publicly displayed or used as a shaming mechanism.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standard 5.2

APST 5.2 (provide feedback). Explanatory post-question feedback targets the student's specific learning gap — enacting the feedback quality Standard 5.2 requires (Wang & Tahir, 2020).

Mathematics — Stage 5, Years 9–10 (organisational infrastructure)

All Stage 5 content strands — organisational infrastructure

Free LMS to plan assignments, give feedback, and conduct structured class communication. Not a pedagogical tool in itself — it does not teach mathematics — but it creates the structured, safe digital environment that makes all other digital learning possible. Vidergor and Ben-Amram (2020) emphasise that effective online learning requires structured digital spaces with defined teacher presence.

Strengths

  • Private teacher-moderated environment — safer than open social media platforms
  • Silent differentiation of assignments protects dignity of learners receiving modified tasks
  • Guardian summaries support home-school communication; seamless Google ecosystem

Limitations & Risks

  • Requires home device and internet — equity risk for low-SES students
  • Value depends entirely on quality of content placed within it
  • Google data practices require verification under the Privacy Act 1988

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

FERPA-compliant; no advertising; only school-provisioned accounts permitted — personal Gmail must not be required for class work. All posts are school digital records. Modelling responsible digital communication within Classroom is itself a form of ICT ethics education aligned to Standard 4.5.
⭐ APST Alignment — Standard 4.5

APST 4.5 (use ICT safely, responsibly, ethically). Selecting Classroom over social media reflects principled professional judgement regarding student digital safety (Vidergor & Ben-Amram, 2020).

Part B · Tool 1 · Deep Development

GeoGebra — Year 10 Non-Linear Relationships

NESA Outcome MA5.2-9NA: Connects graphs and equations of parabolas with equations of the form y = ax² + c

📅
Duration
60 minutes
👥
Year Level
Year 10 — Stage 5
📘
NESA Outcome
MA5.2-9NA

Students compose a carefully structured conjecture statement and justification paragraph using specific mathematical terms: vertex, axis of symmetry, coefficient, dilation, direction of opening. Mathematical writing assessed with a rubric conforming to MA5.2-3WM.

Students calculate the effect of parameter changes on key features — vertex coordinates, y-intercept, direction of opening — using both exact and decimal values read directly from the dynamic GeoGebra coordinate display.

Activity: "How Does a Change the Parabola?"

Students open a pre-built GeoGebra applet displaying y = ax² with a slider controlling a from −5 to +5. The coordinate grid, equation, and a table of selected (x, y) values update in real time as the slider moves. Students work through a printed investigation sheet — the technology provides the dynamic mathematical environment; the sheet provides the structured thinking scaffold. As Zulnaidi et al. (2020) found, GeoGebra's visual and dynamic representation of functions significantly supports student achievement when accompanied by structured teacher-designed tasks.

1

Explore — 15 minutes

Students adjust the slider and record observations in a structured table. Guiding questions on the sheet: "What happens when a > 1? When 0 < a < 1? When a is negative? When a = 0?" Students record in precise mathematical language — full sentences required.

2

Conjecture — 10 minutes

Students write two conjecture statements using a sentence frame: "I notice that when a ___, the parabola ___. I think this happens because ___." Sentence frames are removed for Core and Extension learners — they write in their own mathematical voice.

3

Verify — 10 minutes

Students predict what y = 3x² and y = −0.5x² will look like before adjusting the slider to check. They record whether each prediction was correct and revise their conjecture where needed — developing the habit of testing mathematical claims before asserting them.

4

Generalise — Padlet Board — 10 minutes

Each pair posts their finalised conjecture to the class Padlet board. Teacher projects two anonymised responses and facilitates a discussion about which formulation is most mathematically precise — developing the language of mathematical generalisation required by MA5.2-3WM.

5

Apply Independently — 15 minutes

(1) Sketch y = 2x² and y = −x² without technology. (2) Write the equation of a parabola wider than y = x² that opens downward. (3) Explain in one sentence why y = ax² always passes through the origin regardless of the value of a. These apply MA5.2-9NA outcomes directly.

Differentiation Strategies

🔵 Foundation

Observation table and sentence frames partially completed for all phases. Slider increment set to whole numbers only. Teacher checks in individually after Phase 1 before student proceeds to conjecture phase. Key vocabulary word wall displayed throughout.

🟢 Core

All five phases completed as designed. Investigation sheet provides guiding questions but not answers. Students write conjectures without sentence frames and justify using precise mathematical vocabulary from the word wall.

🟠 Extension

Students extend the applet to explore y = ax² + c by adding a second slider for c. They investigate what c controls, generalise the vertex formula, and verify using coordinate geometry — connecting to Stage 5.3 and Stage 6 content independently.

🟣 EAL/D Support

Key vocabulary (vertex, coefficient, parabola, conjecture) on a visual word wall throughout the lesson. Students may draft conjectures in their first language before translating — the teacher is assessing conceptual understanding, not English fluency.

Assessment

Formative: Teacher circulates during Phases 1–2, reading conjecture drafts and asking probing questions. The Padlet board provides whole-class formative data. Summative element: Phase 5 individual tasks are collected and assessed using a single-point rubric aligned to MA5.2-9NA — specifically assessing whether the student can (a) accurately sketch parabolas from equations, (b) correctly identify the effect of parameter a, and (c) justify their reasoning in written mathematical language aligned to MA5.2-3WM.

📖 Scholarly Support

Zulnaidi et al. (2020) conducted a quasi-experiment with 80 high school students and found significant improvements in mathematics achievement when GeoGebra was used as a teaching aid for functions and non-linear relationships — precisely the NESA outcome this activity targets. The constructivist exploration sequence used here aligns with the pedagogical approach endorsed by Ziatdinov and Valles (2022), who demonstrate that GeoGebra-based investigation tasks produce deeper conceptual understanding than static instruction by making mathematical relationships dynamic and student-investigable.

Part B · Tool 2 · Deep Development

Desmos — Year 9 Linear Relationships

NESA Outcome MA5.1-6NA: Determines the midpoint, gradient and length of an interval, and graphs linear relationships

📅
Duration
50 minutes
👥
Year Level
Year 9 — Stage 5
📘
NESA Outcome
MA5.1-6NA

Screen 5: Students write a definition of gradient in their own words and explain why two lines with the same gradient can never intersect — both assessed against MA5.2-3WM mathematical reasoning and justification outcomes.

Screen 4: Students calculate gradient using rise/run from three coordinate pairs, read coordinates from a dynamic graph, and evaluate how changing the gradient value affects the line's angle of inclination — directly assessed against MA5.1-6NA.

Activity: "What Does Gradient Actually Mean?"

Students join a teacher-created Desmos Activity Builder session via a class code displayed on the board. The six-screen activity builds understanding of gradient progressively — from its everyday meaning (steepness) to its precise algebraic definition (rise over run) to its graphical representation as the slope of a straight line. The teacher dashboard is open throughout and actively used to drive whole-class discussion in Screen 6. As Chechan et al. (2023) found, students using Desmos developed enhanced ability to analyse and verify mathematical functions through visualisation — abilities directly applicable to the gradient concepts targeted in this activity.

1

Screen 1 — Card Sort (Literacy)

Students sort six slope descriptions — "steep uphill", "gentle downhill", "flat", "vertical cliff" — and match each to a corresponding line image. Activates prior knowledge and builds the vocabulary students will need for written responses in Screen 5.

2

Screen 2 — Sketch (Diagnostic)

Students sketch what they think a line with "gradient 2" looks like using the freehand draw tool. The teacher dashboard captures every response simultaneously — revealing pre-existing misconceptions before any instruction begins, directly informing the next teaching move.

3

Screen 3 — Slider Exploration

An interactive graph of y = mx with a slider for m from −5 to +5. Students move the slider and answer three structured questions: "What does a positive m produce? A negative m? What does m = 0 give?" Building conceptual understanding before the formal definition is introduced.

4

Screen 4 — Calculate (Numeracy)

Three coordinate pairs provided. Students calculate gradient using rise/run and enter answers. Immediate correct/incorrect feedback with a hint pointing to the rise-over-run formula — available after one incorrect attempt, not immediately, to preserve productive struggle.

5

Screen 5 — Written Response (Literacy)

"Explain in your own words what gradient measures. Why would two lines with the same gradient never cross?" Students type a full-sentence paragraph. The teacher reads all responses on the dashboard before Screen 6 — identifying which students have conceptual versus only procedural knowledge.

6

Screen 6 — Whole-Class Synthesis

Teacher presents two anonymised Screen 5 responses — one accurate, one containing a common misconception. Five-minute facilitated discussion to build a class consensus definition of gradient. Teacher uses dashboard data to decide whether to extend or reteach before the next lesson.

Differentiation Strategies

🔵 Foundation

Screen 4 hints activated by teacher immediately. A further worked example screen provided after Screen 3 for students still unclear on rise-over-run — delivered silently through Desmos teacher controls without drawing class attention.

🟢 Core

All six screens completed as designed. Screen 4 hints available after one incorrect attempt. Teacher circulates and poses probing questions based on Screen 2 sketches and Screen 5 written responses visible on the dashboard.

🟠 Extension

Hidden bonus screen unlocked by teacher: "Two lines have gradients 2 and −0.5. What do you notice? Investigate the product of gradients of perpendicular lines." Introduces the Stage 5.3 perpendicular gradient relationship.

🟣 EAL/D Support

Mathematical vocabulary (gradient, rise, run, slope, coordinate) pre-taught using a visual glossary distributed at lesson start. Screen 5 responses may be drafted in the student's first language — conceptual understanding is assessed, not English fluency.

Assessment

The teacher dashboard provides real-time formative data from all six screens. Screen 2 (sketch) reveals pre-existing misconceptions. Screen 4 (calculation) identifies procedural gaps. Screen 5 (written response) assesses conceptual understanding and mathematical communication aligned to MA5.1-6NA and MA5.1-3WM. The teacher uses Screen 5 data to decide whether to reteach before the next lesson or proceed to graphing y = mx + b. This data-driven instructional decision directly enacts the evidence-based practice required by APST 5.4.

📖 Scholarly Support

Chechan et al. (2023) found a statistically significant improvement in high school students' understanding of function concepts when Desmos was used, with students specifically reporting enhanced ability to analyse functions and verify answers through visualisation — abilities that Screens 2, 3, and 4 of this activity sequence directly develop. Wang and Tahir (2020) found that the quality of in-lesson feedback — not the game elements — is the key variable determining whether digital assessment tools produce genuine learning gains.

Conclusion

Conclusion

The ten tools assessed in this portfolio represent a deliberate selection grounded in pedagogical evidence rather than novelty. GeoGebra and Desmos rank highest because they elevate mathematical reasoning — rather than answer-giving — as the main student activity, with peer-reviewed research backing this up. The two classroom activities designed in Part B illustrate how digital tools can be purposefully integrated into Stage 5 Mathematics lessons with direct alignment to NESA outcomes, literacy and numeracy integration, and differentiation for all students. Across all ten tools, the consistent principle is that technology can only serve learning when the teacher makes informed, ethical, and evidence-based choices regarding its selection and use — which is exactly what the APST Standards demand.
